# How to Review Your Games

Playing without analyzing is like taking a test and never looking at the corrections. You will make the same mistakes forever.

## The 3-Step Analysis Process

### Step 1: Human Scan (No Engine!)
Immediately after the game, scroll through the moves.
1.  Identify the **Turning Point**: Where did the game change from equal to winning/losing?
2.  Identify **Critical Moments**: Where did you struggle to find a move?
3.  Write down one thing you think you did well and one mistake.

### Step 2: Engine Check
Now turn on the engine (Stockfish).
1.  **Check the Opening**: Did you play the first 5-10 moves correctly? Compare with an opening database.
2.  **The Blunder Check**: Look for spikes in the evaluation bar.
    *   If eval went from +0.5 to -3.0 in one move, *stop*.
    *   Why is the move bad?
    *   What did you miss? (Tactics, hanging piece, missed check?)
3.  **Missed Opportunities**: Did you miss a forced mate or a free piece?

### Step 3: Takeaway
Derive **ONE** specific lesson.
*   *Bad*: "I played bad."
*   *Good*: "I need to stop moving my f-pawn when my king is uncastled."
*   *Good*: "In the French Defense, I shouldn't block my c-pawn with the knight."

## Common Analysis Traps to Avoid
*   **Obsessing over +0.2 vs +0.4**: Engines care about this. You shouldn't. Focus on blunders and clear mistakes.
*   **Blaming the opponent**: "He played so weirdly!" -> Your job is to punish weird play. If you couldn't, that's on you.
*   **Skipping wins**: You often make terrible moves in games you won. Analyzing wins is crucial.

## Tools
*   **Lichess.org / Chess.com Analysis Board**: Upload your PGN or analyze played games.
*   **OpeningTree**: See where your opening stats differ from masters.
